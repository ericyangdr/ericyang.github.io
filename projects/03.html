Data Cleansing in Grocery Store Sales

Project Overview

In this project, I focus on cleaning and preparing grocery sales data to ensure accuracy and reliability. Clean data is essential for making informed business decisions, and this process helps eliminate inconsistencies, duplicates, and errors.

About the Business

FoodYum is a grocery store chain operating across the United States, offering a variety of products, including produce, meat, dairy, baked goods, and household essentials.

With rising food costs, FoodYum wants to ensure that their inventory remains diverse and affordable for all customers. To achieve this, they need clean and structured data that accurately reflects their product offerings and customer purchases.

The Dataset

The dataset comes from the products table and contains customer purchase records from the past year.

Why Data Cleansing Matters

Step 2: Data Cleaning Column by Column

To ensure the accuracy of the analysis, it's essential to clean each column of the dataset. Specifically, I am focusing on the year_added column to ensure that all data aligns with the expected format.

Before diving into the analysis, I first wanted to verify that the product data matches the predefined criteria. I wrote a query to check for discrepancies in the data, ensuring it adheres to the required structure. It's important to note that I did not update the original table during this process.

SELECT
    product_id,
    COALESCE(product_type, 'Unknown') AS product_type,
    COALESCE(NULLIF(REPLACE(brand, '-', ''), ''), 'Unknown') AS brand,
    COALESCE(ROUND(CAST(REGEXP_REPLACE(weight, '[^\d.]', '', 'g') AS DECIMAL(10, 2)), 2), ROUND((SELECT PERCENTILE_DISC(0.5) WITHIN GROUP (ORDER BY CAST(REGEXP_REPLACE(weight, '[^\d.]', '', 'g') AS DECIMAL(10, 2))) FROM products), 2)) AS weight,

COALESCE(
    TO_CHAR(CAST(price AS DECIMAL(10, 2)), '9999999999.99'),
    TO_CHAR(CAST((SELECT PERCENTILE_DISC(0.5) WITHIN GROUP (ORDER BY price) FROM products) AS DECIMAL(10, 2)), '9999999999.99')
) AS price,

    COALESCE(average_units_sold, 0) AS average_units_sold,
    COALESCE(year_added, 2022) AS year_added,
    COALESCE(UPPER(stock_location), 'Unknown') AS stock_location
FROM products;

Step 3: Data Analysis Using Aggregate Functions

To understand the price range for each product type, my manager requested that I determine the minimum and maximum price for every product category.

To achieve this, I wrote a query to return the product_type, min_price, and max_price columns for each product, providing insight into the pricing variation across different categories.

SELECT product_type,
	   MIN(price) AS min_price,
	   MAX(price) AS max_price
FROM products 
GROUP BY product_type;
