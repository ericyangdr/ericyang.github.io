2. Data Importing and Validation

The original dataset consists of 15,000 rows and 8 columns.

Week Column

This column contains integer values ranging from 1 to 6, representing the corresponding weeks, with no missing values. No modifications were necessary.

Sales Method Column

Initially, this column contained five unique values (Email, Call, Email + Call, em + call, email) instead of the expected three. The inconsistencies were caused by variations in capitalization and abbreviations. After standardizing the values, the column now correctly reflects the three intended sales methods: Email, Call, and Email + Call.

Customer ID Column

This column consists of unique identifiers for each customer and had no missing values. No adjustments were necessary.

Revenue Column

A total of 1,074 values were missing in this column. To address this, I replaced the missing values with the mean revenue for each respective sales method.

Years as Customer Column

While this column had no missing values, two rows contained incorrect data. Since the company was founded in 1984, the maximum possible value should be 39 years. However, two entries had values of 47 and 63, which were corrected and replaced with the maximum possible value of 39.

Number of Site Visits Column

This column had no missing values, and all data was valid. No changes were necessary.

State Column

This column contained 50 unique values, corresponding to all U.S. states, with no missing values. No modifications were necessary.

After completing the data validation and cleaning process, I also checked for duplicate rows and found none. The cleaned dataset now consists of 15,000 rows and 8 columns, with no missing values.

def replace_null_revenue(row):
    """
    Replaces null (NaN) values in the 'revenue' column of a pandas DataFrame with the mean (or median) revenue
    for the corresponding 'sales_method' group.

    Parameters:
    -----------
    row : pandas Series
        A single row of a pandas DataFrame containing the 'revenue' and 'sales_method' columns.

    Returns:
    --------
    float
        The value of the 'revenue' column for the given row, either the original value if it is not null, or
        the mean (or median) revenue for the corresponding 'sales_method' group if it is null.
    """
    if pd.isnull(row['revenue']):
        return mean_revenue_by_sales_method[row['sales_method']]
    else:
        return row['revenue']

4. Defining Business Metrics to Measure Performance – Average Revenue per Customer Sales Effort (ARPSE)

To better evaluate the effectiveness of each sales method, I introduced a metric called Average Revenue per Customer Sales Effort (ARPSE). This metric accounts for the time and effort required for each method, offering a more balanced comparison of performance.

For this analysis, sales effort values are assigned as follows:

  • Email → 0.5
  • Email + Call → 1
  • Call → 3

The ARPSE formula is:

ARPSE = (Total Revenue for Method) / (Number of Customers × Sales Effort)

By using ARPSE, we can determine how efficiently each sales method generates revenue in relation to the effort invested, allowing for a clearer performance comparison.

# Define the sales effort for each sales method
sales_effort = {
    'Email': 0.5,
    'Call': 3,
    'Email + Call': 1
}

# Group the data by sales_method and aggregate the total revenue and number of customers
grouped_data = sales_data.groupby('sales_method').agg({'revenue': 'sum', 'customer_id': 'count'}).reset_index()

# Calculate ARCPSE for each sales method
grouped_data['ARCPSE'] = grouped_data.apply(lambda row: row['revenue'] / (row['customer_id'] * sales_effort[row['sales_method']]), axis=1)

# Display the ARCPSE for each sales method
print(grouped_data[['sales_method', 'ARCPSE']])

The ARPSE results show the following:

  • Email achieved the highest ARPSE value at 194.25,
  • Email + Call followed at 183.65,
  • Call had the lowest ARPSE value at 15.86.

These results suggest that the Email method is the most efficient in terms of revenue generated per unit of effort.

However, it is important to consider additional factors:

  • The Email + Call method not only resulted in more website visits but also led to a higher average number of items purchased.
  • This method showed a consistent increase in revenue over the six-week period, which highlights its potential for long-term success.

In contrast, while the Call method generated strong revenue in Week 1, it experienced a steady decline over time.


Conclusion and Recommendations

Based on the comprehensive analysis, it is recommended to prioritize the Email + Call method for the following reasons:

  • While Email has the highest ARPSE, the Email + Call method fosters greater customer engagement, as shown by higher website visits and an increased number of items purchased. This engagement is likely to strengthen customer relationships and drive long-term growth.
  • The Email + Call method has demonstrated a consistent upward trend in revenue over the six-week period, highlighting its potential for sustained success.
  • On the other hand, while the Call method showed early success, it is the least efficient in terms of effort versus revenue and has shown a downward revenue trend over time.

In conclusion, the Email + Call method should be prioritized in future sales efforts, as it offers better customer engagement, steady revenue growth, and a more sustainable balance of effort and return.
