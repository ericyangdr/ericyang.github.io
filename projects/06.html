Warehouse Sales vs. Retail Sales Analysis

Background

This project analyzes warehouse and retail sales data to evaluate the efficiency and contribution of sales channels, item types, and suppliers. The goal was to identify trends and patterns in sales performance over time and across categories. The dataset was sourced from GitHub, which I found while searching for data to practice my Excel analysis skills. I used Excel for the entire process, from data cleaning to visualization. You can download the full data HERE .

Objectives

The key objectives of this analysis were:

  1. To evaluate the efficiency and contribution of sales channels (retail vs. warehouse), item types, and suppliers.
  2. To analyze trends and patterns in sales performance over time and across categories.
  3. To identify underperforming areas and provide actionable recommendations for improvement.

Data Cleaning & Transformation

The first step was to prepare the data for analysis. Here’s how I approached it:

  1. Removing Duplicates: I used Excel’s “Remove Duplicates” tool to ensure there were no duplicate entries in the dataset.
  2. Handling Missing Values: I applied filters to identify and address missing values, ensuring the dataset was complete.
  3. Formatting Columns: I standardized the data by changing the format of sales columns to currency and adding a new column for “Total Sales” calculations.
  4. Linking Sheets: I linked multiple sheets to build pivot tables for deeper analysis.

This process was challenging, especially when dealing with missing values, duplicates, and formatting errors, but it was essential to ensure the accuracy of the analysis.

Data Analysis

To achieve the objectives, I asked myself the following questions:

  • Which sales channel (retail or warehouse) performs better for each item type?
  • Which suppliers contribute the most to sales?
  • Are there underperforming item types in retail or warehouse channels?
  • How do total sales fluctuate month-to-month in 2020?
  • What is the monthly performance of retail vs. warehouse sales?
  • Which item types generate the highest total sales?
  • What is the balance between retail sales and transfers?

Using pivot tables, I analyzed:

  • Total sales by channel
  • Total sales by month
  • Total sales by product
  • Top 10 supplier sales
  • Monthly retail vs. warehouse sales
  • Monthly retail sales vs. retail transfers
  • Highest and lowest sales products

Insights Explained

Here are the key findings from the analysis:

  1. Total Sales: The total sales for the period were $1,028,499, with warehouse sales contributing $824,276 and retail sales contributing $204,233. Retail transfers accounted for $196,910.
  2. Monthly Performance: Sales data was available for four months (January, March, July, and September). July had the highest sales, followed by January, while September had the lowest sales.
  3. Product Performance: Beer was the highest-contributing product, followed by wine. Dunnage was the lowest-performing product.
  4. Retail Sales vs. Transfers: Retail sales and retail transfers were almost equal, indicating a balanced flow of goods.
  5. Supplier Contribution: Anheuser-Busch Inc. was the top-contributing supplier. The top 10 suppliers were identified and included in the dashboard.

Dashboard (Visualization)

I created two dashboards to present the insights clearly:

  1. Sales Overview Dashboard:
    • KPIs: Four cards displayed key metrics—Total Sales, Warehouse Sales, Retail Sales, and Retail Transfers.
    • Visuals: Line charts for sales by month, bar charts for sales by channel and item types, and a column chart for retail sales vs. retail transfers.
    • Slicers: Added slicers on the left side for interactive filtering.
  2. Contribution Overview Dashboard:
    • Visuals: Pie charts for contribution by sales channel and item types, and bar charts for the top 10 suppliers.

All visualizations were designed to be clean, coherent, and easy to understand, following best practices learned from my training.

Recommendations

Based on the analysis, here are my recommendations:

  1. Focus on High-Performing Products: Beer and wine are the top-selling products. Allocate more resources to these categories to maximize revenue.
  2. Optimize Underperforming Products: Dunnage had the lowest sales. Investigate why and consider strategies to improve its performance, such as promotions or bundling with high-demand products.
  3. Leverage Top Suppliers: Strengthen relationships with top suppliers like Anheuser-Busch Inc. to ensure consistent supply and negotiate better terms.
  4. Improve Warehouse Efficiency: Since warehouse sales dominate, ensure warehouses are operating at peak efficiency to maintain this performance.
  5. Boost Retail Sales: Although retail sales are lower than warehouse sales, they are still significant. Implement targeted marketing campaigns to increase retail sales.
  6. Seasonal Strategies: July and January are high-performing months. Plan inventory and marketing efforts to capitalize on these peak periods.
